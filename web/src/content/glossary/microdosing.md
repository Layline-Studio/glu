**Microdosing** GLP-1s means using doses **lower than the standard [titration](/glossary/titration) schedule** — for example, staying at a starter dose long-term, or using small amounts to manage maintenance or minimize side effects. It's a widely discussed community practice, and one to understand carefully because the enthusiasm around it runs ahead of the evidence.

## Why people do it

The motivations are understandable:

- **Maintenance** — staying at the lowest dose that holds their weight and keeps [food noise](/glossary/food-noise) quiet, rather than the maximum.
- **Side-effect control** — a lower dose often means less nausea and fewer GI effects.
- **Cost and supply** — stretching medication further during [shortages](/glossary/supply-and-shortages) or expensive stretches.

Some of this overlaps with legitimate, prescriber-directed practice: settling on a lower [maintenance dose](/glossary/maintenance-dose) is a real and reasonable strategy.

## The honest caveats

Where "microdosing" gets risky is when it becomes DIY:

- **It's largely unstudied.** The trials that established these drugs used defined dose schedules; low-dose, long-term, or self-adjusted regimens don't have that evidence base. "It works for people online" isn't the same as demonstrated.
- **Self-measuring invites errors.** Microdosing often means drawing small amounts from [compounded](/glossary/compounded-semaglutide) multi-dose vials — precisely the setup where dangerous dosing mistakes have happened.
- **Off-label and unsupervised.** Adjusting your own dose outside a prescriber's plan removes the safety checks the schedule is designed around.

## The reasonable version

If a lower dose appeals — for maintenance, tolerability, or cost — the sound path is to **raise it with your prescriber** and adjust under their guidance, with accurate dosing and a [log](/glossary/titration). That captures most of the benefit people chase, without the self-experimentation risk. Microdosing as a supervised strategy: defensible. Microdosing as solo guesswork with a syringe: not.

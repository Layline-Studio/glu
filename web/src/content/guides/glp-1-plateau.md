Somewhere between month six and month eighteen, most people on GLP-1 medication meet the flat stretch: same shots, same habits, unmoving scale. The internet's diagnosis is usually "it stopped working." The trial data says something more useful: **this is what the medication working looks like, later in the curve.**

## First: is it actually a plateau?

Day-to-day weight swings by one or two kilograms on water, sodium, carbohydrate stores, hormones, and digestion timing. None of that is fat. Rules of thumb:

- **A fluctuation** is any story shorter than ~3 weeks, or any comparison between two single weigh-ins.
- **A [plateau](/glossary/weight-loss-plateau)** is a flat *weekly-average trend* over 4+ weeks.

This is the strongest argument for weighing on a routine and looking only at the trend line (Glu plots exactly this): most "plateaus" people panic over are ten days of noise, and the panic produces exactly the diet-thrash that creates real problems. Check the trend before diagnosing anything.

## Why real plateaus happen — even at full dose

Look at the published weight curves in STEP 1 (semaglutide) and SURMOUNT-1 (tirzepatide): rapid loss early, progressive flattening, then essentially **stable average weight from roughly week 60 onward**. Participants with perfect trial adherence plateaued. The average *endpoint* — about 15% loss on semaglutide 2.4 mg, about 21% on tirzepatide 15 mg — *is* the plateau, arrived at and held.

The mechanics: as weight falls, a smaller body needs fewer calories, and **metabolic adaptation** lowers energy expenditure a bit further than the size change alone predicts. Meanwhile the medication's appetite effect is constant, not escalating. Intake and expenditure eventually re-meet, and weight stabilizes at the new equilibrium. The drug hasn't quit; the easy arithmetic has been spent.

## What your options actually are

### 1. Reframe — maybe nothing is wrong

If you've lost meaningful weight and the trend is flat, you may simply be *at* your result — the part of the curve every trial participant reached. The clinical question becomes maintenance: protecting the loss (see [rebound weight gain](/glossary/rebound-weight-gain)), not forcing more. For a lot of people the honest move at the plateau is to stop treating it as a problem and start treating it as an arrival. Compare against your starting point, not against the loss rate of month two — and check what else moved: bloodwork, blood pressure, clothes, stamina, [food noise](/glossary/food-noise). The scale is one instrument on the dashboard.

### 2. Check the dose ceiling

If you plateaued at a mid [titration](/glossary/titration) dose, there may be headroom — moving from 1 mg toward 2.4 mg semaglutide, or 5 mg toward higher tirzepatide steps, produced clearly larger average losses in the dose-response data. Whether the next step is worth its side effects is a prescriber conversation, and it's a far more productive one when you arrive with a dose history and a weight trend instead of a feeling. A months-long flat trend at a sub-maximal dose is precisely the chart that changes prescriptions.

### 3. Audit the quiet drift

Appetite suppression commonly softens as months pass, and eating drifts upward without announcing itself — portions creep, calorie-dense convenience returns, the medication keeps the *noise* down while intake rises anyway. Two honest weeks of meal logging usually settles whether drift is part of your plateau. Same for movement: weight loss itself reduces daily calorie burn (you're carrying less), so activity that outran your intake a year ago may only match it now.

### 4. Strength-train into the plateau

A plateau is the ideal moment to shift goals from *losing* to *recomposing*: resistance training plus adequate protein preserves or builds muscle at stable weight, which improves body composition and modestly supports metabolism — and it gives progress a scoreboard the scale can't show (see [muscle loss on GLP-1s](/guides/muscle-loss-on-glp-1)).

### What doesn't work

Crash-restricting on top of the medication (accelerates [lean mass loss](/glossary/lean-mass-loss) and is unsustainable), skipping doses to "reset sensitivity" (no evidence, real regression risk — the gaps just lower your medication level; see [missed doses](/guides/missed-glp-1-dose)), and scale-watching daily with rising anxiety (measurement noise, treated as news).

## When a plateau plus symptoms means something else

A flat scale *with* returning hunger and food noise at full dose, or weight *regain* while on medication, is a different conversation than a quiet plateau — bring it to your prescriber. And an abrupt appetite return after a pharmacy switch is worth mentioning too; supply chains have produced real dose inconsistencies.

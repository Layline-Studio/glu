The bathroom scale is a single, noisy number — and on a GLP-1, trusting it alone is a recipe for both false panic and false comfort. It can't tell fat from muscle from water, so it misreads [plateaus](/guides/glp-1-plateau), hides [muscle loss](/guides/muscle-loss-on-glp-1), and turns normal fluctuations into fake emergencies. Here's the fuller dashboard that actually tells you whether treatment is working.

## Start with the scale — but read it right

Don't abandon the scale; use it correctly. Weigh on a **routine** (same day, same conditions) and watch the **trend line**, not individual readings. Day-to-day swings of a kilogram or two are water, sodium, and hormones — not fat. A flat *trend* over 4+ weeks is a real [plateau](/glossary/weight-loss-plateau); a flat *week* is just noise. Most scale-driven misery comes from reacting to noise as if it were signal.

## Body measurements: where recomposition shows up

A tape measure sees what the scale can't. Measure your **waist, hips, and arms** monthly, same conditions each time. A shrinking waist at a stable weight is [body recomposition](/glossary/body-recomposition) — losing fat while keeping muscle — which the scale reports as "nothing happened." Waist circumference is also a better marker of the metabolically risky visceral fat than weight alone.

## Strength benchmarks: proof you're keeping muscle

This is the most underused progress metric on a GLP-1, and one of the most important. Because rapid weight loss risks [lean mass](/glossary/lean-mass-loss), you want evidence you're holding onto muscle. Simple benchmarks:

- **Push-up count** (or wall/knee push-ups)
- **Comfortable carry weight** (groceries, a loaded bag)
- **Sit-to-stand reps** in 30 seconds

Flat-or-rising strength during significant weight loss is strong evidence you're losing the *right* kind of weight — exactly the outcome the [protein and resistance-training](/guides/muscle-loss-on-glp-1) work is aiming for.

## Health markers: the point of the whole thing

For many people the most meaningful wins never touch the scale. From your regular lab visits, track:

- **[A1C](/glossary/a1c)** — average blood sugar, the key diabetes marker.
- **Blood pressure** and **cholesterol/lipids**.
- **Liver markers** (often improve with fat loss).

Watching A1C or blood pressure fall over successive lab draws is both medically important and genuinely motivating — a [non-scale victory](/glossary/non-scale-victory) with real health behind it.

## The human metrics

Don't discount the things that don't come with a number:

- **Energy** — stairs without stopping, longer walks.
- **Clothing fit** — often what others notice first.
- **Quieter [food noise](/guides/what-is-food-noise)** and a calmer relationship with eating.
- **Better sleep, less joint pain, improved mood.**
- **Progress photos**, monthly, same conditions — sometimes the most convincing evidence of all.

## Put it on one dashboard

The reason to track several signals isn't busywork — it's accuracy. When the scale stalls, your measurements, strength, and bloodwork tell you whether you've plateaued or you're recomposing. When you're anxious about a bad weigh-in, the trend line talks you down. Keeping weight, measurements, symptoms, and habits in one place (this is precisely what Glu is built for) turns "is this working?" from a daily gut-check into a question your own data can answer.
